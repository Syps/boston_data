Poverty and Crime in Boston

January 1 - August 10, 2015

Two topics we discussed in class this semester were poverty and crime. Thanks to class content, I feel I understand these issues more and better relate to people whose lives are deeply impacted by them.

I also learned how little I know about my home for the past few years: Boston. I’ve always been vaguely aware that poverty and crime are issues here as in other cities, but that was really the extent of my knowledge. So, I thought I’d use data to understand the situation more fully.

In the charts below, I’ve visualized several pieces of 2015 crime and poverty data from the US Census Bureau and the City of Boston. I learned a lot in the process of making them, and hope this site can further educate others.


I was surprised to see that Roxbury has the highest rate of violent crime in Boston, considering its proximity to Northeastern. Perhaps more surprising however, was Mission Hill’s high poverty rate and its relatively low crime rate.

I tried to investigate this further by mining BPD FIO data. Unfortunately, the FIO dataset does not include exact location data, so it is impossible to see which FIO activity took place in Mission Hill. My guess is that the low crime rate is a product of gentrification. Many students have moved to Mission Hill, pushing existing residents out. In light of this trend, it is possible that Northeastern pushes BPD to police more heavily there.

As we discussed in class, the chart suggests a positive relationship between poverty and crime, though this trend isn’t illustrated as strongly as shooting data.


Shootings - January to August 2015

Here, the dichotomy between poor and wealthy Boston neighborhoods is better illustrated. We can see that the top 3 poorest neighborhoods from above (Dorchester, Mattapan, Roxbury) have higher rates of shootings than any other neighborhoods. For Dorchester and Roxbury, the difference is astronomical (8- and 6-fold, respectively).


The following heatmap illustrates the distribution of poverty and crime across Boston’s geography. Use the selector on top to pick criteria for the heatmap, then hover over a neighborhood to reveal stats.


By playing with the map, it is interesting to see that the Boston neighborhoods where I and many other Northeastern students hang out the most (Back Bay, Downtown, Chinatown) all have the lowest poverty rates. It thus becomes clear how even in a city as small as Boston, the distribution of poverty and gentrification create a divide in perceptions of the city. Residents of communities like Roxbury and Dorchester consistently experience some degree of poverty or its effects, while student and Yuppie communities can remain fairly oblivious.


Take for instance, the calendar heat maps below, which plot violent crimes committed from January to August 2015 in three different communities. Brighton and Roxbury have relatively equal populations, but lie on different ends of the economic spectrum. Fenway is a smaller community right next to Northeastern home to many students such as myself.

Most days on the first two calendars are white, meaning no violent crimes occurred. On the other hand, only 9 days occurred in which no violent crimes were reported in Roxbury over the 8 month period. The heat map coloring is also relative to each neighborhood. This means that the darkest square for Brighton indicates a day where 3 crimes were committed, whereas 13 crimes were committed in Roxbury’s darkest square.


A discussion we had in class is also of note here. Specifically, we discussed how poor communities with higher crime rates generally have less trust of the police and report crimes less frequently. Meanwhile, it is not hard to image that residents of Brighton and Fenway will likely report a violent crime if they see one. So the difference may be even more significant than the heat maps suggest.


Conclusion

Exploring and visualizing this data was a meaningful experience for me, but I recognize that I only scratched the surface. Given more time, I would have really like to continue exploring the socioeconomic differences between neighborhoods and there is a lot more data to do so. The US Census bureau has a lot to offer and I look forward to seeing the City of Boston’s data portal continue to add new information. I will likely continue to play with this site and hopefully further increase my understanding of these topics.
